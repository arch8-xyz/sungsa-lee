Welcome to the House of Stories

Imagined as a community living-knowledge ecosystem with a museum at its heart, 'sungsa lee'(or house of stories) aims to document, collate, preserve and nurture the Lepcha identity, history and practices while promoting sustainable livelihood through responsible tourism in Lower Dzongu.

While led by Seela Abhigail Lepcha, this initiative has wholeheartedly been supported by, driven by, physically built by, and run by the community of Gnon-Sangdong. From donations of land to building materials to food to labour to love - the community volunteers and champions this project through a collaborative and transparent process, and it continues to take shape through their efforts.

This project is supported by Royal Enfield and GreenHub.

Tung Buk

Tungbuk is a well known Lepcha musical instrument. Many Lepcha songs are carried over the deep, woody notes of the Tungbuk and resonate across the mountains.

Tung-buk or Tumbok is carved out of a single piece of soft wood. Animal hide, commonly goat hide, is stretched over the face of the rounded, hollowed out base. 3 holes are punctured into the hide for the base reverberations.

A tung-buk typically has 3 strings, but 4-stringed versions are not uncommon. The strings are attached to a corresponding number of tuning pegs at the head and tightened over a bridge. In the past, these were made of a fibre pulled from stinging nettle, but these days are usually made of nylon. The instrument is finger plucked or uses a bamboo plectrum.

A woven band maybe attached to the tungbuk for it to be hung over the shoulder, much like a guitar strap.

The 4- stringed tungbuk pictured here belongs to Kinga Lepcha from Gnon.

Sumok Thyaktuk

Sumok thyaktuk is the traditional Lepcha bell-shaped hat. It was the official headgear of the royal soldiers the regime of Chogyal dynasty in Sikkim, and wearing Sumok thyaktuk is still in practice even today. It is considered an important part of the Lepcha identity and is worn during marriage ceremonies, Lepcha and Buddhist festivals, and by community elders.

Sumok thyaktuk is one of the oldest documented material crafts of the community. The hat is intricately hand woven with thin strips of Ru or cane (Calamus acanthospathus) and Po-young or small bamboo (Cephalostachyum capitatum). A Sumok thyaktuk requires finess and craftsmanship and thus it can take upto a month to weave a single hat!

Lufu Phengbo

Lufu Phengbo is a collection of various wild animals and birds strung together and used by Lepcha monks, bumthing/ bongthing, for spiritual and medicinal purpose for curing various diseases. A Lufu Phengbo can only be possessed and used by a practioner and it is passed down from bumthing to bumthing only, each time one is reincarnated. A Bumthing may add their own elements to the collection as they see appropriate.

This Lufu Phengbo belongs to Tshring Lepcha (Bumthing in Sangdong). It contains the dried foot of a chicken, a part of the shell of the tortoise, shells, beak of a vulture among other things.

Rung gow

Rung Gow is a stone grinder used mainly for making millet flour. It is commonly placed in the outer courtyard of the Lepcha house, Ronglee. Coarse millets or cereals are placed between the to stones and ground to powder by rotating the top stone using the wooden handle.

Bamphok or Bentok

The traditional Lepcha knife

A Bentok is a common utility knife that has multiple everyday usages. It has an iron blade with a wooden handle and is encased in a wood sheath. Most Lepcha tie their Bentok around their waist as they go about their day in the field, in the forest or at home to keep it handy. The Bentok is handy for cutting grass in the field, and also for cutting firewood, twigs, branches, splitting bamboo and cane, and also for meat.

This bentok belongs to Naku Tshering Lepcha in Gnon. The woven strap carries the Lepcha weave and colours.
